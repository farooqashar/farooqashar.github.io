Sloan Law Spring 2021 FAQs

We hope that pulling together information on all the Sloan-based law courses for this spring will be useful if you are thinking about a law course.  Here is what we think you need.

 

1.  WHAT ARE THE CHOICES?

There are 3 Sloan-based law courses being offered this Spring.  All are fully remote; all meet full-semester; and all are subject to MIT’s special grading rules for Spring 2021.

  • 15.615.  Essential Law for Business (a.k.a. the “Business Essential” course).  Instructors:  John Akula and Lou Rodriques.  M/W 8-930am.  9 units.  Letter-graded.  This the most broad-gauged and covers the most material.  It provides the strongest foundation for a career involving management responsibilities, complex transactions, or finance.
  • 15.618/6181.  Entrepreneurship, Innovation, Startups and the Law (a.k.a. the “Startups” course). Instructor:  John Akula.  Th 7-8:30pm.  6 units.  P/D/F.  This course covers the key legal issues faced in launching, financing, growing, and exiting from a new venture based on an innovative technology. Students considering launching a venture or working in the startup world, or who are working with a technology with commercial potential, will find this course of special value.
  • 15.622/6221. The Law of AI, Big Data and Social Media; and other Digital Tech Hot Spots (a.k.a. the “Digital Tech” course).  Instructors:  John Akula and Lou Rodriques.  Tu 7-8:30pm.  This is a deep dive into the increasingly robust legal response to innovations and applications involving AI, big data and data science, social media, and related developments.  If you are planning a venture or a career built upon cutting-edge skills in this area, this course is designed for you.

 

2.  WHAT IF YOU ARE NOT A SLOANIE?  WHAT IF YOU ARE AN UNDERGRAD?  WHAT IF YOU SKIPPED THE SLOAN BIDDING SYSTEM?

  • These courses are designed to be appropriate for ALL MIT students.  No prior knowledge of law or business is expected.  The Startups course and the Digital Tech course both draw a large proportion of their students from outside Sloan.
  • Undergraduates from all departments are welcome in all the courses.   For the Startups course, undergraduates must register under 15.6181; and the for Digital Tech course undergraduates must register under 15.6221.  (The Business Essentials course will have an undergrad “meets with” listing next year.)  In each of the past 2 years more than 50 undergrads have in fact enrolled.
  • You can enroll even if you skipped the Sloan bidding system.  All the courses are remote this spring and we won’t be turning away any interested MIT students, irrespective of the official “capacity.”

 

3.  WHAT IF YOU WANT TO BE A LISTENER?   

Sorry.  We have a No-Listener policy for students enrolled in a degree program, unless the course is P/D/F and your program does not allow P/D/F courses to be credited to your degree, or unless you are taking a second Sloan-based law course.

 

4.  WHAT IF YOU WANT TO JOIN A COURSE LATE?  

Consistent attendance is essential in all the law courses.  But classes will be recorded and arrangements will be made for any late-arrivers to view videos of missed sessions.  However, if it is after March 1st, a request to join one of the courses will be evaluated on a case-by-case basis.

 

5. WHO ARE THE INSTRUCTORS?   

The instructors are both based at MIT Sloan.  We both practiced law, and like to think that gives a practical and realistic tilt to the way we discuss legal risks and opportunities.  But now we both just teach.  John’s faculty web page is here, and Lou’s is here.

The Canvas sites for all of the courses will be open to the MIT community for the first few weeks of the semester.  And you can reach out to either of us with any further questions.
